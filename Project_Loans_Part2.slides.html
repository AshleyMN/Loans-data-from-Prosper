Part II - Factors Influencing the Outcome of a Loan Amount¶

by Ashley Muthoni¶

Investigation Overview¶

My goal during the investigation was to look at the factors that influence how much money a borrower can be issued in relation to its monthly payment. I wanted to look at the categoric variiables of loan status, employment status and loan term as well as additional factors like the lender yield and income range.

Even with the loan amount and its monthly payment having a strong positive correlation, borrowers wtih favourable employment statuses seem to also ocassionaly face uncertainity in remitting payments towards their higher-priced loans. It was interesting to note that borrowers who take out lower loan amounts in the 12 month term were at more at risk of also being deliquent or being in default.

Dataset Overview¶

The dataset consisted of the original loan amounts and other related attributes of 113937 loans. The attributes included the loan amount and its monthly payment, loan status, employment status, term, income range and its verification. Fifty-eight variables were eliminated because of missing values and their relevance to the analysis.

Distribution of Loan Amount¶

Loan Amount in the dataset take on a very large range of values, from about $1000 at the lowest, to about $25,000 at the highest. Plotted on a logarithmic scale, the distribution of the original loan amount prices takes on a multimodal shape.

Distribution of Monthly Loan Payment¶

Monthly Loan Payment in the dataset take on a very large range of values, from about $20 at the lowest, to about $1000 at the highest. Plotted on a logarithmic scale, the distribution of the monthly loan payments takes on a unimodal shape.

Monthly Loan Payment by Loan Status and Term for Selected Loan Amounts¶

We can see how monthly loan payments differs across loan status for different loan terms on selected loan amounts: those on the upper end from $10,000 to $15,000 and lower ends of $1000 to $5000. For both upper and lower amounts, as the longer loan terms effects a decreased monthly loan payment.

For loans ranging from those current to the defaulted and chargedoff, it is more favorable to take long-term loans of 60 months. This has lower risk levels for borrowers regardless of the loan amount they apply for.

Monthly Loan Payment by Employment Status and Term for Selected Loan Amounts¶

Reproducing the same plots with Employment Status and Terms instead of Loan Status grade shows the same trend of decreased monthly payments with longer term loans. However despite having an attractive status of being employed, self-employed, full time; contributing to that 12-month loan still has the uncertainity of servicing it. 60-month long term loans still prove to be the most favourable loan outcomes regardless of employment status.

Loan Amount by Income Range and its Verification¶

It is evident that borrrowers within higher income brackets with the backing of documentation have more favourable loan outcomes compared to their counterparts who did not provide them. The mean difference between the two is minor with the former having a wider spread and even outliers which tells of a broader scope of money that can be loaned out.

Loan Amount and Lender Yield by the Investors of random sample¶

High loan amounts are not necessarily funded by more investors as even fewer investors still fund high-end loan amounts. There appears to be a minimum loan amount of $1000. This funding however, does not automatically translate to higher returns from the investment for the investors because of other factors surrounding the structuring of the loan.

Examples
--------

    The simplest way to use nbconvert is

            > jupyter nbconvert mynotebook.ipynb --to html

            Options include ['asciidoc', 'custom', 'html', 'latex', 'markdown', 'notebook', 'pdf', 'python', 'rst', 'script', 'slides', 'webpdf'].

            > jupyter nbconvert --to latex mynotebook.ipynb

            Both HTML and LaTeX support multiple output templates. LaTeX includes
            'base', 'article' and 'report'.  HTML includes 'basic', 'lab' and 
            'classic'. You can specify the flavor of the format used.

            > jupyter nbconvert --to html --template lab mynotebook.ipynb

            You can also pipe the output to stdout, rather than a file

            > jupyter nbconvert mynotebook.ipynb --stdout

            PDF is generated via latex

            > jupyter nbconvert mynotebook.ipynb --to pdf

            You can get (and serve) a Reveal.js-powered slideshow

            > jupyter nbconvert myslides.ipynb --to slides --post serve

            Multiple notebooks can be given at the command line in a couple of 
            different ways:

            > jupyter nbconvert notebook*.ipynb
            > jupyter nbconvert notebook1.ipynb notebook2.ipynb

            or you can specify the notebooks list in a config file, containing::

                c.NbConvertApp.notebooks = ["my_notebook.ipynb"]

            > jupyter nbconvert --config mycfg.py

To see all available configurables, use `--help-all`.
